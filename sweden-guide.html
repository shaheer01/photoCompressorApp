The Swedish Pension System

PillarNameContributionHow It WorksYou Control?
1st Pillar Allman pension (Public) 18.5% of salary (auto) 16% goes to inkomstpension (pay-as-you-go), 2.5% to premiepension (you choose funds) Only premiepension fund selection
2nd Pillar Tjanstepension (Occupational) ~4.5-30% depending on employer Employer pays into pension fund via collective agreement (ITP/SAF-LO). Huge part of total pension. Sometimes fund selection
3rd Pillar Privat pension (Private) Voluntary ISK account, regular savings, stocks, funds. No longer tax-deductible since 2016. Full control
Important: Sweden's pension replaces only ~50-60% of your final salary. The tjanstepension from your employer is crucial. Always check that your employer has a collective agreement (kollektivavtal) — it guarantees pension contributions, insurance, and better terms.

Money-Saving Tips

Use Store Apps

ICA, Coop, and Willys apps have personalized discounts. ICA's bonus system gives 1% back on purchases.

Shop Late Evening

Many stores mark down items 30-50% after 18:00. Bread, meat, and dairy near expiry are heavily discounted.

Asian & Middle Eastern Stores

Rice, spices, vegetables, and tofu are 30-60% cheaper at ethnic grocery stores compared to ICA/Coop.

Buy Frozen

Frozen vegetables and berries are much cheaper than fresh, often equally nutritious, and reduce waste.

Karma & Too Good To Go

Apps that sell surplus food from restaurants and stores for 50-70% off. Great for trying new places cheaply.

Meal Plan Weekly

Plan meals before shopping. Average Swedish family wastes ~8,000 SEK/year on thrown-away food.

Work Permits & Employee Rights for Non-EU Workers

Critical information for non-EU/EEA workers about your rights, contract clauses, and what employers can and cannot do under Swedish law.

New Law: June 1, 2026 — Major Work Permit Reform

Sweden's work permit system was overhauled effective June 1, 2026 (Bill Prop. 2025/26:87). Key changes: work permits are no longer tied to a single employer — you can change jobs by notifying Migrationsverket within 14 days (no full reapplication needed). The unemployment grace period is now 6 months (up from 3) for those with 2+ years on a permit. The minimum salary threshold has been raised to 90% of the median salary (~29,680 SEK/month).

Can an employer lock you in for the duration of your work permit?

No. Under Swedish law (LAS — Lagen om anställningsskydd), an employee can always resign with the applicable notice period (1–6 months depending on tenure). An employer cannot contractually force you to stay for 2 years (or any fixed duration) simply because they sponsored your work permit. Such clauses are unenforceable under Swedish labour law and may constitute a form of labour exploitation. As of June 2026, you no longer even need to reapply for a new permit — simply notify Migrationsverket within 14 days of changing jobs.

QuestionAnswer Under Swedish Law (Updated June 2026)
Can I switch employers on a work permit? Yes — and it's now easier than ever. Since June 1, 2026, work permits are no longer tied to a single employer. You can change jobs by notifying Migrationsverket within 14 days of the change. The new role must meet the salary threshold (≥29,680 SEK/month) and qualification requirements. No full reapplication needed.
Can my employer add a "stay for X years" clause? Such clauses are not enforceable. Swedish law guarantees the right to resign with the standard notice period (typically 1–3 months). No contract can override this fundamental right under LAS. The 2026 reform further weakens any employer leverage, as switching jobs no longer requires a new permit application.
What about non-compete clauses? Non-compete clauses exist but are strictly regulated. They are typically limited to 9–18 months, must involve compensation (usually 60% of salary during the restriction period), and only apply to senior/specialist roles with access to trade secrets. They cannot prevent you from working entirely.
Can my employer charge me for the work permit costs? No. The employer bears the cost of the work permit application. Charging the employee for visa/permit costs is a red flag for labour exploitation.
What if my employer threatens to revoke my permit? The employer cannot "revoke" your work permit — only Migrationsverket can. If your employment ends, you now have a 6-month grace period (if you've held a permit for 2+ years; 3 months otherwise) to find new employment. During this time, you can legally stay in Sweden and job-search. After 3 months, you must show you can support yourself financially.
Signing bonus clawback — is that legal? Yes, within reason. An employer may offer a signing bonus with a repayment clause (e.g., repay if you leave within 12 months). This is different from restricting your right to leave — you can still resign, but you may owe back the bonus. The amount must be reasonable.
Training cost repayment clauses? Employers may include clauses requiring repayment of training costs if you leave within a certain period. These are generally enforceable if the training was expensive, specific, and clearly agreed upon in writing — but they cannot be used to effectively trap you in employment.
What salary must my employer pay? Sweden has no statutory minimum wage, but for work permit holders, Migrationsverket requires that the salary is at least 90% of the median salary (raised from 80% as of June 2026) — currently ~29,680 SEK/month before tax. Exempted groups (shortage occupations, recent graduates, healthcare professionals, qualifying startups) need only meet 75% of the median.
Do I need health insurance? New requirement (June 2026): If your stay is ≤1 year, you must have or have applied for comprehensive health insurance valid for the employment period. For stays over 1 year, you're covered by Swedish public healthcare once registered with Skatteverket.
Can Migrationsverket reject my permit due to my employer? Yes (new rule). Since June 2026, Migrationsverket can reject work permit applications based on employer shortcomings — e.g., crimes committed by the employer, suspected offences, or sanctions imposed on them. Research your employer before accepting an offer.
How do I get permanent residence? Standard work permit holders: after 4 years of employment (within the last 7 years) you can apply for permanent residence.
EU Blue Card / researchers / doctoral students: only 3 years needed (reduced from 4, effective June 11, 2026).

Requirements have tightened for all applicants: you must prove income self-sufficiency (≥29,680 SEK net/month), demonstrate Swedish language proficiency (A2–B1 level via SFI or equivalent), and pass an integration assessment. Permanent residence may become time-bound (5-year renewals with periodic audits) instead of lifelong.
Key timeline (updated June 2026): Work permit no longer tied to single employer (notify Migrationsverket within 14 days of job change) → 6-month grace period if unemployed after 2+ years → Permanent residence after 4 years for standard work permits (3 years for EU Blue Card / researchers / doctoral students) — requires language proficiency + income proof.

Red Flags in Employment Contracts

  • "You cannot leave for X years" — Unenforceable under Swedish law. You can always resign with notice.
  • "We will revoke your visa if you quit" — Employers cannot revoke visas. You get a 3-month grace period.
  • "You must repay your work permit costs" — Illegal. The employer pays for the permit.
  • Salary below kollektivavtal level — Grounds for permit denial/revocation by Migrationsverket.
  • No written employment contract — Employers must provide a written contract within 1 month (by law).
  • Passport confiscation — Highly illegal. Report immediately to police (Polisen: 114 14).
